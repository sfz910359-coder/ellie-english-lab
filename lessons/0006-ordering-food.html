0006 — Ordering Food & Drinks

🍁 Canada Survival⏱ ~20 min🎯 A2→B1🇩🇪→🇬🇧 German speaker

You walk into a Tim Hortons. The smell of coffee and donuts hits you. Behind the counter, someone says: "What can I get for you today?" This is not a test. This is breakfast. But if you freeze, you end up with nothing — or something you didn't want. Today we fix that.

In Canada, ordering food is the most common social interaction you will have. Cafés, fast food, restaurants — every day, multiple times. Master these 10 phrases and you eat like a local.

Du gehst in ein Tim Hortons. Kaffeeduft und Donuts. Hinter der Theke: "What can I get for you today?" Das ist kein Test — das ist Frühstück. In Kanada ist Essen bestellen die häufigste soziale Interaktion. Jeden Tag, mehrmals. Diese 10 Phrasen — und du bestellst wie ein Einheimischer.
你走进一家 Tim Hortons。咖啡和甜甜圈的香味。柜台后有人说："您今天要点什么？"这不是考试——这是早饭。在加拿大，点餐是你最频繁的社交互动。每天好几次。掌握这10句话，点餐就像本地人。

🍁 Canadian Coffee Words (you NEED these)

  • double-double = coffee with 2 creams + 2 sugars (only at Tim Hortons — say it anywhere else and they'll stare at you)
  • regular = 1 cream + 1 sugar
  • black = no milk, no sugar
  • washroom = toilet/bathroom (always "washroom" in Canada, never "toilet" — that sounds crude)

🔍 Grammar Spot — "I'd like" vs "I like"

This is a tiny difference that German speakers miss constantly:

You sayThey hearProblem
"I like a coffee."I generally enjoy coffee as a concept❌ (sounds weird)
"I'd like a coffee."I want a coffee right now please✅ (correct!)

I'd like = I would like = ich hätte gern (eine Bestellung). I like = ich mag (allgemein). Nur drei Buchstaben Unterschied — komplett andere Bedeutung.

I'd like = ich hätte gern (Bestellung, Wunsch). I like = ich mag (allgemeine Vorliebe). "I like a coffee" klingt für Muttersprachler seltsam — als würdest du Kaffee als Konzept mögen. Sag immer I'd like beim Bestellen.
I'd like = 我想要（点餐）。I like = 我喜欢（泛指）。"I like a coffee"英语母语者听着很怪——好像你喜欢咖啡这个概念。点餐永远说 I'd like。

📖 Story Snippet — "The First Sip"

The coffee was too hot. I burned my tongue on the first sip and almost dropped the cup. Outside the window, someone was walking a dog — a big, fluffy thing that looked like a bear. The cashier laughed. "First time at Tim's?" she asked. "Is it that obvious?" I said. She grinned. "You get used to it. By Friday, you'll be ordering a double-double without thinking." I wasn't sure about the double-double part. But sitting there, holding a burning-hot cup in a country I'd just arrived in — that felt pretty good.
Der Kaffee war zu heiß. Ich verbrannte mir die Zunge und hätte fast den Becher fallen lassen. Draußen ging jemand mit einem Hund spazieren — ein großes, flauschiges Ding, das wie ein Bär aussah. Die Kassiererin lachte. "Zum ersten Mal bei Tim's?" "So offensichtlich?", sagte ich. "Man gewöhnt sich dran. Bis Freitag bestellst du double-double ohne nachzudenken." Ich war mir beim double-double nicht sicher. Aber da zu sitzen, einen brennend heißen Becher in der Hand, in einem Land, in dem ich gerade angekommen war — das fühlte sich ziemlich gut an.
🔊 Listen — story:
Ordering food is not about grammar. It's about rhythm. You walk in. You say the thing. You pay. You eat. The cashier doesn't care if you said "I'd like" or "Can I have." They care that you were friendly and fast. So practice the rhythm more than the words. 🥯
